The Wissembourg Priscian Glosses

Digital Edition - Introduction

The digital edition of the Wissembourg Priscian Glosses uses the critical edition of Priscian’s Institutiones Grammaticae published by Martin Hertz in 1855 under the direction of Heinrich Keil as a reference (Grammatici latini ex recensione Henrici Keilii, 7 vols; vol. 2: Prisciani Institutionum grammaticarum libri I–XII; vol. 3: Prisciani Institutionum grammaticarum libri XIII–XVIII, Leipzig: Teubner, 1855).

Alessandro Garcea (Sorbonne Université, Paris) generously provided us with his TEI-XML transcription of Hertz’ edition.

The digital edition is structured according to the pages and the lines of Hertz’ edition. In order to enable comparison with other digital editions of Priscian glosses (see: Gloss Corpus), we have followed the model provided by the “St Gall Priscian Glosses”-project. In particular, we link to their scans of Hertz’ edition at the beginning of each new page, we reproduce their adaptation of Hertz’ edition with regard to line breakings, we follow them in recording the position of each line within Hertz’ edition. For instance: “II 1, 5” indicates that it begins line 5 of page 1 of volume II of Keil’s Grammatici Latini.

For the sake of comparison with similar digital editions, all lines of Hertz’ edition have been encoded, also those which were not glossed by the Wissembourg glossators. The line breakings of the MS Wolfenbüttel, Herzog August Bibliothek, 50 Weiss. [hereafter: MS 50 Weiss.] obviously do not conform with those of Hertz’ edition: they are signaled in italic and within round brackets.

Differences between Priscian’s text as contained in Hertz’ edition and in the MS 50 Weiss. have been recorded as “text variations”. Omissions in the MS 50 Weiss. are marked as follows: “Codex 50: omitted”.

Interventions on the primary text are listed underneath the related lemma of Hertz’ edition and preceded by the entry: “Codex 50”. The interventions analyzed thus far can be divided into four categories: a) emendations; b) rubrications; c) reference signs; d) glosses.

  1. All modifications of the primary text (expunctions, rasurae, additions) made by the main scribe are marked as “emendations”.
  2. The decorative interventions on Priscian’s text as contained in MS 50 Weiss. limit themselves to red initials and fillings: these stem probably from the main scribe and have been recorded as “rubrications”.
  3. The “reference signs” (also called reference marks or signes de renvoi) occurring in the MS 50 Weiss. are of two sorts. Rarely we encounter reference signs connecting two words of the primary text: these are lemma-lemma signs, which had the function of marking the recurring of the same topic within Priscian’s grammar, thus facilitating orientation within its long sentences. More often, reference signs were used to link a lemma to its related marginal annotation: these are lemma-gloss signs. In these cases, the scribe drew the signs both above the lemma and at the beginning of the corresponding explanation. The digital edition provides the user with an image of each reference sign occurring in the manuscript in order to facilitate future studies on the shape and the spread of such graphic symbols.
  4. Construe marks, annotation signs, interlinear and marginal annotations are recorded under the category of “glosses”. According to their function and content, glosses are divided into several groups. The taxonomy adopted here reproduces in part that provided by Rijcklof Hofman, The Sankt Gall Priscian Commentary. Part 1 (2 vols, Münster, 1996). In particular, construe marks are regarded as “synctatical glosses using symbols“, since they aim at facilitating comprehension by highlighting the synctactical structure of a sentence and its word order. The digital edition contains an image of each annotation sign and construe mark occurring in the MS 50 Weiss. Construe marks consist mostly of dots. The images will be published with the next release.

The digital edition provides the user with information about the position of each intervention on the manuscript page (symbol: ), about the writer responsible for the intervention (symbol: ) and about the type of intervention (symbol: ). In the next release of the edition, every intervention will be linked with its corresponding image detail on the manuscript page.

Three writers have been identified thus far: the main scribe, who was responsible for the writing of the primary text and probably also of the construe-marks; the first glossator, who can be identified with Otfrid of Wissembourg; and the second glossator, who wrote his annotation quite at the same time as Otfrid. Both glossators wrote a limited amount of Old High German words. These can be spotted within the edition and are also listed separately. New findings are highlighted through brown side bars.

The glosses have been encoded relying upon the digitized images of the MS 50 Weiss. available in the digital library of the Herzog August Bibliothek in Wolfenbüttel. Some annotations are hardly legible on the screen and will be checked on the real manuscript before project end.

The edition is a work in progress and will be completed until 2025. A search mask will be implemented with the next release.
